Synopsis When the multi-coloured GrandPrix grid comes under starter's orders, a £250-million industry is about to be unleashed before an annual TV audience of 45 billion viewers.  These colossal figures are matched by shattering performances of the cars and the unstinting efforts of the teams themselves.

Usually, just three or four of the major names - Williams, Benetton, Ferrari, McLaren - dominate the racing.  But, working just as hard, a dedicated core of smaller teams is doing battle in the hope that one day they, too, will become Grand Prix winners.  These single-minded competitors may not make the headlines but without them Grand Prix would lose its depth and an essential part of its attraction.

Who are these people, struggling against impossible odds?  Where are they from?  What drives them on?  How do they compare with the famous racers doing fortnightly battle at the front during a fast and furious summer on 16 tracks around the world?

<i>Inside Formula One 1996</i> pays tribute not just to the elite of motor racing, but to the promising, the professional, and the poores - by F1's affluent standards - of Grand Prix entrants.

The 11 teams are united by a love of motor sport at its highest level.  They are also aware of dangers which are not in the least selective.  A struggling outfit at the back of the grid can suffer the devastating consequences of an accident just as much as a team which is favourite to win the championship.  At times like that, background and budget are irrelevant.  It is a terrible part of a never-ending struggle, which is why the story of each team must be told.

The penetrating photographs, supported by detailed narrative, are the work of Jon Nicholson and Maurice Hamilton; a unique combination from another winning team.
