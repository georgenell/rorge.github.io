Synopsis According to a profile in <i>The Guardian</i>, Mary Midgley is 'the foremost scourge of scientific pretension in this country: someone whose wit is admired even by those who feel she sometimes oversteps the mark.'  Considered one of Britain's finest philosophers, Midgley is notorious for exposing the illogical logic of a science turned poor doctrine.  Never more at home than when taking on the high priests of evolutionary theory - Dawkins, Wilson and their acolytes - she famously described evolution as 'the creation-myth of our age'.  In <i>Evolution as a Religion</i> she examines how science comes to function as a substitute for religion, and then exposes is as nothing more than a magnificent sham.  As even, her argument is flawlessly insightful: a punchy, compelling, lively indictment of the notion of science as a religion.  Both the book and its author are true classics of our time.
