Synopsis An excerpt from the Mt Algidus story by Sally Nell<blockquote>We arrived in the spring of 1985 with Charles, eight weeks old, Victoria, two years, George, five years, and Lucy, six and a half years old.  The snowdrops were out around Mona Anderson's house.  Every spring we were there I looked forward to seeing the snowdrops, that meant we were on the way to summer.  Though that also meant floods.

Springtime was also shearing time.  We had decided to continue with blade shearing that first spring, as Chris Richards had done before us.  Graham was to class the wool himself and we were aware of costs so I got to be shearers' cook, which was my job for all the time we were at Algidus.  I always had help, though some better than others.

In 1985 it was Angela Milne who was employed to teach Lucy and George their correspondence school work and to help me as needed.  We shore for fifteen days straight that first spring.  The routine was get up, cook and wash dishes.  If we got a load of nappies on the line that was a plus.  We fell into bed at the end of the day!

Usually, before the men came to the house for tea, we managed a quick gin and tonic.  That's if the food was all prepared.  Angela was once going to write about her adventures at Algidus and call it 'Gin and Cabbages'.  We must have had a lot of cabbages that year!  Angela now lives in London and still talks of gin and cabbages and what fun it was.

With shearers, farm staff and family, we had at times twenty-plus to feed.  Most of the time I believe they had enough and went away full.  We planned a BBQ one night, after days of sun, only to pick the first day of rain!  The men still cooked the chops just under the veranda.  Another time I cooked a roast with a tea towel stuck to the bottom of it.  The tea towel must have stuck to the frozen meat when I was transferring it into the roasting pan.  The meat was still well cooked so I didn't tell the men about that one!

The shearers were always great audiences for the children's plays and concerts and we had a lot of parties.  One year we even had a budding poet whi spent many days writing a poem for us, about Algidus.

Because the shearing shed could be seen from the house, I had a few minutes for last minute panics as the men walked up.  Like the time the pie top didn't cook, so I turned the grill up high, removed the uncooked pastry, stuffed the food processor with bread and left over toasties and scones from morning smoko, gave it a quick whiz and covered the pie.  Back in the oven for a quick grill and lunch was ready!</blockquote>
