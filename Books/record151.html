Synopsis The Great Democracies is the fourth and final volume of Sir Winston Churchill's history.  In it he takes the history of the English-speaking peoples from the days of peace which followed the downfall of Napoleon to the death of Queen Victoria and the begining of our present era.  In the century which this volume covers, the many streams, the source and wanderings of which Sir Winston has traced with such illuminating care in his earlier volumes, come together into the mighty river which we see flowing onward towards its destiny.  The British Empire at its highest peak; the creation of the Dominions and the first steps towards the British Commonwealth of Nations; the industrial revolution; the startling expansion of the United States and their rise to world power, interrupted only by the savage slaughter of the Civil War: these events alone have made the nineteenth century one of the most decisive in the history of mankind.  But at the end, from all these changes and developments, one stands out as a towering peak.  Gone are the dynastic wars, gone the internecine struggles on the European continent which, for all their smallness, could disrupt the balance of the civilized world.  When this volume ends the map is world-wide, and across it are spread in friendship, in the brotherhood of a common language, enjoying an alliance which cannot and, indeed must not ever be broken - the great democracies.  These democracies have fought for and risen to a common ideal: the right of the common man to live his own life and to express himself freely.  Today these rights are assailed by many enemies; Sir Winston's story of their final emergence is a timely reminder of their great importance.
